Draft Contaminant Candidate List 6 (CCL 6) — PFAS as a class
Pre-rulemaking · public comment open
EPA Office of Water · SDWA § 1412(b)(1)(B)
EPA's draft CCL 6 (Federal Register, April 6, 2026) includes PFAS as a class as one of four chemical groups identified for possible future drinking-water regulatory consideration under SDWA. Not an enforceable rule; CCL placement signals continued evaluation for occurrence, health effects, monitoring, and potential future regulation. EPA is seeking public comment before finalizing the list. 📅 Comment period closes June 5, 2026 (docket EPA-HQ-OW-2026-0055). PFAS are carried forward as a structural group using the EPA/TSCA definition — notably narrower than the OECD definition, which means some structurally distinct PFAS may fall outside CCL 6 consideration. Final CCL 6 expected to be signed by November 17, 2026. Pairs with UCMR 5 (occurrence monitoring) and the NPDWR (future class-based regulation).

EPA 2026 Interim Guidance on Destruction & Disposal of PFAS
Interim guidance · non-binding
EPA OLEM · Updated 2026 from 2024 baseline
April 28, 2026 update — EPA published FR Doc 2026-08174 opening 60-day public comment on the updated 2026 Interim Guidance (superseding the April 2024 version). The 2026 guidance evaluates and ranks destruction/disposal technologies by release potential for the first time, covering high-temperature incineration, smoldering combustion, electrochemical oxidation, supercritical water oxidation, permitted Class I underground injection wells, permitted RCRA Subtitle C hazardous-waste landfills, and landfill containment. EPA Administrator Zeldin committed to annual updates going forward. The guidance explicitly identifies key data gaps on byproduct formation (PFAS thermal decomposition products, incomplete destruction residues) and release-pathway uncertainties for landfill leachate and biosolids co-management. Non-binding; does not establish requirements. Comment period closes June 29, 2026 · Docket EPA-HQ-OLEM-2020-0527. Why this matters for scientists: remediation engineers designing PFAS waste disposal trains for CERCLA sites, DoD installations, AFFF-laden wastewaters, ion-exchange resins, and granular activated carbon now have an updated federal benchmark — and the technology ranking framework will shape vendor contract specifications and technology selection justification in RODs and FSPs.

RCRA Listing — 9 PFAS as Hazardous Constituents
Final-rule stage · pending publication
EPA OLEM · 40 CFR 261 Appendix VIII
🚨 Update — May 8, 2026: EPA withdrew its separate February 2024 proposed rule that would have revised RCRA's "hazardous waste" definition for corrective action (FR Doc 2026-09179). Withdrawal narrows the regulatory hook for PFAS investigation at RCRA-permitted TSDFs — though CERCLA §106, RCRA §§7003 and 3013 authorities remain. The Appendix VIII listing proposal below remains pending.
Proposes adding PFOA, PFOS, PFBS, HFPO-DA, PFNA, PFHxS, PFDA, PFHxA, PFBA (and salts/structural isomers) to Appendix VIII as hazardous constituents (not hazardous wastes). Would not by itself impose cradle-to-grave hazardous-waste controls but would affect RCRA corrective action at TSDFs — EPA identified ~1,740 potentially affected facilities. The Unified Agenda listed an April 2026 final-rule target; the final rule has not yet been published in the Federal Register. Biggest near-term remediation lever.

TSCA Section 8(a)(7) PFAS Reporting
Window locked · substantive rule pending
EPA OPPT · 40 CFR 705
May 2026 update — TSCA Low-Volume Exemption (LVE) policy shift: EPA announced it will largely halt use of TSCA's LVE pathway when evaluating new PFAS for approval (LVE applies to substances at ≤10,000 kg/year, allowing streamlined review). EPA also plans to work with industry to withdraw certain prior LVE approvals for PFAS. New PFAS introductions will require full TSCA §5 premanufacture notice review — raising the evidentiary bar for fluorinated alternatives, semiconductor materials, and specialty chemical PFAS substitutes.
Requires anyone who manufactured/imported PFAS 2011–2022 to report identity, uses, volumes, exposure, disposal. Final April 13, 2026: EPA moved the TSCA 8(a)(7) PFAS reporting start date to Jan 31, 2027, or 60 days after the effective date of the forthcoming final substantive revision, whichever is earlier. EPA is retaining the six-month reporting period structure for now (with an additional six months for qualifying small article importers), but the concrete end dates depend on the new start date and the forthcoming final rule timing.

EPA OW · CWA Section 405
Draft assessment, not enforceable limits. EPA released the Draft Sewage Sludge Risk Assessment for PFOA/PFOS on Jan 14, 2025; public comment period closed Aug 14, 2025. PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation (NPDWR)
Final
EPA Office of Water · 40 CFR 141 · Final April 10, 2024
Enforceable MCLs: PFOA & PFOS at 4.0 ppt; PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA at 10 ppt; Hazard Index = 1 for PFHxS/PFNA/HFPO-DA/PFBS mixture. 🚨 Week of May 18, 2026: TWO proposed rules now published in the Federal Register — comment periods open (typically 60 days).
  • FR Doc 2026-10086 / 91 FR 29425 (EPA rule page) — Compliance Deadline Extension. EPA proposes a federal exemption under SDWA §1416(f) and §1450(a)(1) extending PFOA/PFOS MCL compliance from April 26, 2029 to April 26, 2031 for systems that opt in. Systems with PFOA or PFOS results at or above 12 ppt during the extension must implement short-term exposure-reduction actions — not a blanket delay. 24 PDF pages.
  • FR Doc 2026-10085 / 91 FR 29413 (EPA rule page) — Rescission of Regulatory Determinations. EPA proposes to rescind regulatory determinations for PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA (GenX), and the Hazard Index mixture (these three plus PFBS) under SDWA. 13 PDF pages.
Concurrent D.C. Circuit challenge in AWWA v. EPA; outcomes could affect the scope, timing, or continued validity of some or all PFAS NPDWR requirements.
Implementation support: EPA's PFAS OUT initiative will proactively contact ~3,000 water systems with known PFOA/PFOS challenges, beginning with systems above 4 ppt in summer 2026. PFAS OUT connects systems to funding and technical-assistance resources but does not itself provide direct funding or technical assistance.

OLEM Engagement on Low-Level PFAS in Soil
Pre-rulemaking · engagement complete
EPA Office of Land & Emergency Management (OLEM)
EPA's OLEM completed six engagement sessions (April 27 – May 12, 2026) with developers, industry, environmentalists, states, tribes, and local governments on managing surface soils with low concentrations of PFAS. EPA is now compiling feedback and evaluating options for guidance, screening levels, or other practical pathways. Stakeholders are pushing EPA to define what qualifies as "low-level" PFAS in soil — a threshold below which strict cleanup may not be required. Directly relevant to site investigation, redevelopment, and remediation work as more sites trigger PFAS detection under the 2024 CERCLA PFOA/PFOS hazardous-substance designation. AGCA's Leah Pilconis (general counsel) publicly called for "clear risk-based thresholds." Sen. Shelley Moore Capito (R-WV, Senate EPW chair) has pressed EPA for action. Several states have moved ahead: NJ sampled PFAS in surface soil statewide (below interim cleanup standards); NY is developing soil standards using background-levels study; MN just updated its 2026 SRVs using MDH toxicity values.

PFAS Vapor Pathways & Vapor Intrusion
Emerging · watch item
ITRC + state-level activity · No federal PFAS-specific VI guidance yet
Vapor-forming PFAS (notably fluorotelomer alcohols (FTOHs), perfluoroalkyl sulfonamides, and ultrashort-chain PFAS including TFA and PFPrA) have been detected in soil gas, sewer gas, outdoor air, and indoor air — but a complete subsurface-to-indoor-air VI pathway has not yet been conclusively demonstrated. The 2015 OSWER VI Technical Guide remains the operative federal framework; no federal PFAS-specific VI screening levels exist.
Recent developments worth tracking:
  • ITRC — Jan 2026 Fact Sheet "Vapor-forming PFAS" (PDF) — formal technical synthesis of vapor-forming PFAS and VI pathway considerations, knowledge gaps in vapor pressure / Henry's law data, and inhalation toxicology needs.
  • Hawaii DOH — only state with interim PFAS VI screening levels (two subsurface vapor + six indoor air screening levels, including for TFA and PFPrA). Interim guidance does not yet require PFAS-VI assessments but signals direction.
  • Michigan EGLE — Air Quality Workgroup (page) conducts PFAS air-quality investigations at AFFF / industrial sites; no codified PFAS-VI screening levels yet.
  • Analytical method development — vapor-phase PFAS sampling for soil gas, sewer gas, and indoor air now described in the peer-reviewed literature (Schauer et al., RSC Environ. Sci. Atmos., 2025).
Why this matters for scientists: Integral risk assessors and site-characterization teams supporting AFFF sites, chemical manufacturing facilities, and PFAS-impacted redevelopment should be aware that ITRC has formalized the vapor pathway as a credible-but-unproven concern. Expect inquiries from clients about whether to add soil gas or indoor air to PFAS sampling plans; the analytical methods now exist, even where screening levels do not.

PFAS Use At Data Centers Emerges As New Litigation Focus, Attorney Says
LitigationMay 14, 2026InsideEPA · PFAS News
How data centers use PFAS: two technically distinct pathways — fluorinated cooling gases (atmospheric release; GWP thousands × CO&sub2;) and PFAS immersion-cooling fluids (potential water-pathway release). U.S. Chamber of Commerce 2024 report identified data centers as one of 7 critical PFAS-reliant industry sectors, but disposal and wastewater pathways have received little study. Source & signal: Eve Gartner (Earthjustice) flagged data centers as a next-wave PFAS site category during a May 13 Environmental Law Institute webinar launching ELI's March 2026 report "Current Trends in Toxics Litigation." Implications for science work: new site type for environmental site assessment, fate-and-transport modeling, and PFAS characterization; expect demand for source-attribution and exposure-pathway analyses. EPA announced last year it's prioritizing TSCA new-chemical reviews for data-center and AI projects — though unclear if existing PFAS in cooling get the same scrutiny. State of the field: ELI's Cecilia Diedrich predicts PFAS litigation will surpass asbestos in scope and cost; causation (long latency, science gaps) remains the central scientific question. Forward-looking: CIEL's Patrick Boyle expects microplastics to follow the same trajectory.

EPA's New Biosolids Guide May Tier Risks Based On PFAS Concentrations
📌 PinnedFederal PolicyMay 11, 2026InsideEPA · PFAS News
OW Assistant Administrator Jessica Kramer announced May 7 (U.S. WASH Convening, D.C.) that EPA is developing new guidance on PFAS in biosolids — explicitly framed as correcting confusion from the Biden-era PFOA/PFOS draft risk assessment, which industry, states, farming groups, and water systems criticized for overestimating risk via non-real-world modeling. New guidance will use "real-life scenarios" and signals a tiered-concentration approach aligned with Michigan, Minnesota, Virginia, and Maryland (NACWA's model template: ≥100 µg/kg PFOA+PFOS triggers land-application ban + source-reduction program; ≥50 µg/kg triggers source-reduction + reduced application rate of 3 dry tons/acre). The Biden draft RA comment period closed August 2025; EPA is still reviewing comments. Practical signal: directional shift toward a less restrictive federal posture on biosolids land application, prioritizing the highest-concentration material. Pinned: foundational evergreen reference — will shape state programs, remediation cost-recovery, and any future federal biosolids rulemaking.
🔄 Update — late May 2026: A senior EPA official subsequently confirmed the agency has convened an internal work group on PFAS in biosolids and is coordinating with USDA to inform both the draft RA review and the forthcoming tiered guidance. The EPA-USDA coordination is a meaningful signal at the seam where biosolids land-application policy gets shaped — expect federal direction to integrate agricultural-use considerations with the tiered-risk framework. Maine — Title 38 §1614
Active
Broadest state PFAS law in the U.S. · 38 MRS § 1614

Product stewardship clients in cookware, small-appliance, food-contact, and container categories should reassess product scope against the broader MDEP interpretation.

EU Directive 2026/805 — Water Framework PFAS Standards
In force May 11, 2026
Amends Water Framework Directive + EQSD + Groundwater Directive
📌 Pinned EU Directive 2026/805 entered into force on May 11, 2026, establishing the EU's first numeric PFAS standards for both surface water and groundwater. Groundwater quality standard: 0.0044 µg/L (4.4 ppt) for the sum of four priority PFAS (PFHxS, PFOS, PFOA, PFNA) — well below U.S. federal MCLs. Surface water environmental quality standard: a group of 25 PFAS expressed as PFOA toxic equivalents, reflecting cumulative-assessment frameworks. Also adds trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) — a PFAS degradation product of fluorinated pesticides and refrigerants — to regulated substance lists for the first time, and introduces effect-based monitoring assessing combined risk. Member states must transpose by December 22, 2027; national compliance with new standards required by 2039. Why this matters for scientific staff: the 4.4 ppt sum-of-four groundwater standard is substantially more stringent and analytically more demanding than current U.S. federal standards — relevant for multinational facility site assessments, international clients, and analytical method development.

EU PPWR — Food-Contact Packaging PFAS Ban
Final · effective Aug 12, 2026
Regulation (EU) 2025/40 · Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation
Regulation (EU) 2025/40 takes full effect August 12, 2026, banning intentionally added PFAS in food-contact packaging placed on the EU market across all 27 member states. No grandfathering provision — even packaging manufactured before August 2026 cannot be sold after. Concentration thresholds: 25 ppb per individual PFAS (targeted analysis, excluding polymeric PFAS), 250 ppb for the sum of targeted PFAS, and 50 ppm total fluorine (including polymeric PFAS). Covers greaseproof paper, pizza boxes, baking trays, microwave packaging, food wrappers, and similar items. Why this matters for scientists: the dual-pathway analytical regime (targeted PFAS screening + total-fluorine screening) creates a new analytical protocol demand, and the 50 ppm TF threshold may catch fluoropolymer coatings previously considered low-concern. U.S. manufacturers and exporters selling packaged food to the EU must have reformulated or substituted packaging before this date; downstream certification and testing burden falls on the supply chain. Product stewardship specialists should flag this as an imminent hard deadline distinct from the broader REACH restriction timeline.

EU REACH Restriction — PFAS in Firefighting Foams
Final · phased 2026–2035
Regulation (EU) 2025/1988 · amends Annex XVII of REACH
Adopted October 2025. Phased restriction on PFAS in firefighting foams at ≥1 mg/L (sum of all PFAS). First operative deadline — October 23, 2026: bans PFAS-containing firefighting foams placed on the market in portable fire extinguishers and requires labelling on all PFAS-containing foam agents sold after that date. October 23, 2030: ban extends to all firefighting foam uses. SEVESO industrial sites retain phased transitions through October 2035. Why this matters for scientists: the 1 mg/L sum-PFAS threshold is a new analytical trigger distinct from MCL or groundwater standards. EU facility operators with portable extinguisher inventories must restock with PFAS-free alternatives before October 2026; AFFF site investigators supporting international clients should track these phase-in dates alongside U.S. MDL and DoD guidance. PFAS foams are the primary contamination driver at ~60% of contaminated firefighting sites across Europe.

🔒 InsideEPA Emerging theory: data centers as defendants
May 14, 2026Novel theory of liability
Data centers are being publicly flagged as a next-wave PFAS site category. Why this matters for scientists: data centers use PFAS in two technically distinct ways — fluorinated cooling gases (atmospheric release; GWP thousands × CO&sub2;) and immersion-cooling fluids (potential water-pathway release). W.L. Gore Motion to Dismiss GORE-TEX PFAS Class Action (D. Del., April 22, 2026)
Apr 2026Consumer fraud class action · Motion to dismiss
W.L. Gore & Associates moved April 22, 2026 to dismiss a putative consumer class action (D. Del.) brought by 18 plaintiffs from 18 states alleging GORE-TEX sustainability claims (incl. "PFC* Free Laminate") were misleading. Gore's central scientific argument: plaintiffs failed to identify which PFAS their specific garments contained, at what concentrations, or how their garments caused harm — instead relying on studies that did not test those products. Why this matters for scientists: mirrors a growing line of dismissals in PFAS consumer litigation where courts reject claims relying on general class-wide PFAS studies rather than product-specific analytical data. Reinforces the evidentiary importance of product-level PFAS analytical data (not just substance identity) in any regulatory or litigation context.
Texas AG Civil Investigation of Lululemon Over PFAS in Activewear (April 13, 2026)
Apr 13, 2026State AG enforcement · Civil investigative demand
Texas AG Ken Paxton on April 13, 2026 served a civil investigative demand on Lululemon USA Inc. to examine whether the company misled consumers about product safety given its sustainability/wellness marketing. The investigation will scrutinize Lululemon's internal Restricted Substances List, product testing protocols, and supply chain disclosures. Why this matters for scientists: extends the state-AG enforcement pattern from cosmetics into athletic/performance fabrics. Supply-chain chemical disclosures and restricted-substance testing programs will face increasing scrutiny even in states without enacted PFAS-in-products legislation. Clients in apparel, athletic wear, and technical textiles should expect heightened expectations on product-level testing and supplier audit documentation.
Wild Virginia, Inc. v. EPA — CWA §303(d) PFOS Impaired-Waters Suit (E.D. Va. No. 3:25-cv-01043)
May 2026CWA citizen suit · Motion to dismiss briefing
Plaintiff challenges EPA's approval of Virginia's 2024 CWA §303(d) impaired-waters list while taking no action on fish-tissue PFOS data for segments of the Middle Chickahominy River watershed. April 29, 2026: EPA moved to dismiss, arguing no nondiscretionary duty exists under the CWA for agency approval/disapproval of state lists and that the citizen-suit provision does not reach discretionary agency decisions. May 5, 2026: Wild Virginia replied, contending EPA admitted missing the 30-day statutory deadline; seeks vacatur and order requiring EPA to act on the full list. Why this matters for scientists: tests whether fish-tissue PFOS data requires state listing under §303(d) — a threshold that, if settled, would define how PFAS-contaminated aquatic pathways must be addressed in impaired-waters assessments nationally and shape cleanup obligations and TMDL development. Watch for the MTD ruling.
