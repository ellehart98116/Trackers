EPA 2026 Interim Guidance on Destruction & Disposal of PFAS
Interim guidance · non-binding
EPA OLEM · Updated 2026 from 2024 baseline
April 28, 2026 update — EPA published FR Doc 2026-08174 opening 60-day public comment on the updated 2026 Interim Guidance (superseding the April 2024 version). The 2026 guidance evaluates and ranks destruction/disposal technologies by release potential for the first time, covering high-temperature incineration, smoldering combustion, electrochemical oxidation, supercritical water oxidation, permitted Class I underground injection wells, permitted RCRA Subtitle C hazardous-waste landfills, and landfill containment. EPA Administrator Zeldin committed to annual updates going forward. The guidance explicitly identifies key data gaps on byproduct formation (PFAS thermal decomposition products, incomplete destruction residues) and release-pathway uncertainties for landfill leachate and biosolids co-management. Non-binding; does not establish requirements. Comment period closes June 29, 2026 · Docket EPA-HQ-OLEM-2020-0527. Why this matters: remediation engineers designing PFAS waste disposal trains for CERCLA sites, DoD installations, AFFF-laden wastewaters, ion-exchange resins, and granular activated carbon now have an updated federal benchmark — and the technology ranking framework will shape vendor contract specifications and technology selection justification in RODs and FSPs.

TSCA Section 8(a)(7) PFAS Reporting
Window locked · substantive rule pending
EPA OPPT · 40 CFR 705
May 2026 update — TSCA Low-Volume Exemption (LVE) policy shift: EPA announced it will largely halt use of TSCA's LVE pathway when evaluating new PFAS for approval (LVE applies to substances at ≤10,000 kg/year, allowing streamlined review). Effluent Limitation Guidelines (ELGs) for PFAS — industrial wastewater
Proposed · multiple sectors advancing
EPA OW · Clean Water Act § 304(b) · 40 CFR Parts 414, 433, 463, 466, etc.
EPA is advancing PFAS-specific industrial wastewater discharge limits under the Clean Water Act effluent guidelines program. Active rulemakings:
  • OCPSF (Organic Chemicals, Plastics & Synthetic Fibers, 40 CFR Part 414) — proposed rule published January 23, 2025 introducing PFAS effluent limits for facilities that manufacture PFAS. Public comment period closed; EPA reviewing.
  • Metal Finishing & Electroplating (40 CFR Parts 433 + 413) — PFAS limits under consideration; EPA's Effluent Guidelines Program Plan identified these categories for revision.
  • Landfill Leachate (40 CFR Part 445) — preliminary study underway; landfills are a known PFAS pathway via leachate to POTWs and surface water.
  • Centralized Waste Treatment — under EPA review for PFAS handling at CWT facilities (often the destination for landfill leachate and ion-exchange regeneration).
Once finalized, ELGs flow into NPDES permits for direct dischargers and into pretreatment standards for indirect dischargers through POTWs. Why this matters: for industrial wastewater consulting, permit renewals, and treatment-system design, ELGs are the trigger that moves PFAS from "monitor only" to "comply with a numeric limit." Engineers designing treatment trains (ion exchange, GAC, RO, foam fractionation) need to factor in the discharge-limit endpoint, not just the source-control or treatment-feasibility question. Clients in chemical manufacturing, metal finishing, semiconductor, plating, and landfill operations should expect compliance planning to intensify as each ELG finalizes. Pairs with the Biosolids Risk Assessment (POTW sludge endpoint) and CERCLA designation (release reporting + cost-recovery downstream).

PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation (NPDWR)
Final
EPA Office of Water · 40 CFR 141 · Final April 10, 2024
Enforceable MCLs: PFOA & PFOS at 4.0 ppt; PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA at 10 ppt; Hazard Index = 1 for PFHxS/PFNA/HFPO-DA/PFBS mixture. 🚨 Published May 20, 2026: TWO proposed rules now published in the Federal Register — comment periods open (typically 60 days).
  • FR Doc 2026-10086 / 91 FR 29425 (EPA rule page) — Compliance Deadline Extension. EPA proposes a federal exemption under SDWA §1416(f) and §1450(a)(1) extending PFOA/PFOS MCL compliance from April 26, 2029 to April 26, 2031 for systems that opt in. PFAS Vapor Pathways & Vapor Intrusion
Emerging · watch item
ITRC + state-level activity · No federal PFAS-specific VI guidance yet
Vapor-forming PFAS (notably fluorotelomer alcohols (FTOHs), perfluoroalkyl sulfonamides, and ultrashort-chain PFAS including TFA and PFPrA) have been detected in soil gas, sewer gas, outdoor air, and indoor air — but a complete subsurface-to-indoor-air VI pathway has not yet been conclusively demonstrated. The 2015 OSWER VI Technical Guide remains the operative federal framework; no federal PFAS-specific VI screening levels exist.
Recent developments worth tracking:
  • ITRC — Jan 2026 Fact Sheet "Vapor-forming PFAS" (PDF) — formal technical synthesis of vapor-forming PFAS and VI pathway considerations, knowledge gaps in vapor pressure / Henry's law data, and inhalation toxicology needs.
  • Hawaii DOH — only state with interim PFAS VI screening levels (two subsurface vapor + six indoor air screening levels, including for TFA and PFPrA). Interim guidance does not yet require PFAS-VI assessments but signals direction.
  • Michigan EGLE — Air Quality Workgroup (page) conducts PFAS air-quality investigations at AFFF / industrial sites; no codified PFAS-VI screening levels yet.
  • Analytical method development — vapor-phase PFAS sampling for soil gas, sewer gas, and indoor air now described in the peer-reviewed literature (Schauer et al., RSC Environ. Sci. Atmos., 2025).
Why this matters: Integral risk assessors and site-characterization teams supporting AFFF sites, chemical manufacturing facilities, and PFAS-impacted redevelopment should be aware that ITRC has formalized the vapor pathway as a credible-but-unproven concern. Expect inquiries from clients about whether to add soil gas or indoor air to PFAS sampling plans; the analytical methods now exist, even where screening levels do not.

Groundwater quality standard: 0.0044 µg/L (4.4 ppt) for the sum of four priority PFAS (PFHxS, PFOS, PFOA, PFNA) — well below U.S. federal MCLs. Surface water environmental quality standard: a group of 25 PFAS expressed as PFOA toxic equivalents, reflecting cumulative-assessment frameworks. Also adds trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) — a PFAS degradation product of fluorinated pesticides and refrigerants — to regulated substance lists for the first time, and introduces effect-based monitoring assessing combined risk. Member states must transpose by December 22, 2027; national compliance with new standards required by 2039. Why this matters: the 4.4 ppt sum-of-four groundwater standard is substantially more stringent and analytically more demanding than current U.S. federal standards — relevant for multinational facility site assessments, international clients, and analytical method development.

EU PPWR — Food-Contact Packaging PFAS Ban
Final · effective Aug 12, 2026
Regulation (EU) 2025/40 · Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation
Regulation (EU) 2025/40 takes full effect August 12, 2026, banning intentionally added PFAS in food-contact packaging placed on the EU market across all 27 member states. No grandfathering provision — even packaging manufactured before August 2026 cannot be sold after. Concentration thresholds: 25 ppb per individual PFAS (targeted analysis, excluding polymeric PFAS), 250 ppb for the sum of targeted PFAS, and 50 ppm total fluorine (including polymeric PFAS). Covers greaseproof paper, pizza boxes, baking trays, microwave packaging, food wrappers, and similar items. Cavalier et al. v. Apple, Inc. — PFAS Class Action Standing & RSL Precedent (N.D. Cal., March 16, 2026)
📌 PinnedMar 16, 2026Consumer fraud class action · Motion to dismiss denied
In Cavalier et al. v. Apple, Inc. (N.D. Cal.), the court denied Apple's motion to dismiss a PFAS false-advertising class action alleging that fluoroelastomer watch bands contained PFHxA at concentrations exceeding Apple's own supplier-policy thresholds (internal RSL flagged PFHxA "restricted" at 25 ppb; plaintiff testing showed >1,000 ppb). Four holdings with broad implications: (1) Article III standing via representative-product testing (one post-filing lab sample can stand in for the class); (2) health-function products carry implied duty to disclose PFAS regardless of affirmative safety claims; (3) public PFAS phase-out commitments demonstrate knowledge and undercut "reasonable consumer" defense; (4) fraud-based misrepresentation claims survive economic-loss rule where health-risk exposure is alleged. Why this matters: the elevation of internal RSL programs and phase-out targets as primary evidentiary documents — the stringency of a company's own PFAS thresholds can be used against it as the standard of care. For product stewardship work involving wearable tech, outdoor gear, medical device, and personal-care products, internal RSL programs and phase-out targets may interact with product-level PFAS concentrations in this evidentiary framework. Pinned as a foundational precedent.

About this tracker & methodology

This tracker is a curated reference covering federal, state, international, litigation, and industry PFAS developments. It is produced by Library Services at Integral Consulting Inc. The resource is open-source, publicly accessible, and free to share.

This page documents the inclusion criteria, editorial workflow, and AI use behind the tracker. Library Services maintains this resource with transparency about methodology as a core principle.

Inclusion criteria (curation gate)

Items are reviewed against a focused curation gate. Only developments with momentum qualify for inclusion:

  • Rule finalized; regulation entering force
  • Court ruling issued, especially at circuit or appellate level
  • Settlement reached or court-approved
  • Law signed
  • Major guidance or synthesis published
  • Quarterly or mid-year roundup from a credible source

Items that do not pass the gate: bills being drafted (until enacted or substantially advancing), speculative or rumor-stage coverage, courts considering stays, and industry groups weighing petitions. The goal is a dense, scan-friendly reference that holds up week to week.

Trending Now selection. The Trending Now section identifies topic areas with sustained underlying public activity over the recent 30–60 day window. Inclusion is observational, based on volume and recency of public developments documented elsewhere in this tracker, not a judgment about the merits of any party’s position or action. Each Trending item links to the underlying tracker entries for full context.

Editorial workflow (human-in-the-loop)

Every item on the tracker passes through a structured workflow:

  1. Scheduled scans. Four weekly scans run automatically: federal news (Fridays), state agency sweep across 19 states (Fridays), international sweep across EU/UK/Canada/Australia/Germany/Belgium/Brazil regulators (Fridays), and a Monday-morning Federal Register API check for any EPA, HHS/FDA, or DoD PFAS notices posted over the weekend.
  2. Source aggregation. Scans pull candidate items from trade press (InsideEPA, JD Supra, Lexology, National Law Review, law-firm blogs), state attorney general press releases, federal direct sources (Federal Register API, FDA pages, CPSC), and state agency landing pages.
  3. AI-assisted candidate identification. Each candidate is evaluated against the curation gate. AI assistance is used to sift the volume of public sources and produce structured candidate digests for human review. The AI does not render conclusions, characterize parties, or make recommendations.
  4. Human editorial review. The Library Services curator reviews every candidate, verifies the underlying source, applies the curation gate, and decides whether to include the item. Items that pass review are drafted into tracker entries.
  5. Neutrality discipline. Draft language is reviewed against an internal editorial guide that excludes value-laden verbs, administration-era prefixes, prescriptive language, and characterization of parties' conduct. Allegations are framed as allegations.
  6. Pre-publication hygiene check. An automated script verifies structural integrity, tab placement, filter counts, date accuracy, and section descriptions before any save.
  7. Manual final review. The curator does a manual editorial review of the rendered tracker before publishing.
  8. Publication. The updated tracker is pushed to GitHub Pages. The companion weekly digest is generated from the same content.
